Decision Framework

Fork Zero — Buy vs. Lease

Before anything else: Can Neurocrine acquire this campus?

This is not a simple yes/no. It breaks into three parts: financial capacity, structural viability, and board appetite. The answer determines which architecture applies. Every hour spent on partner selection and lease structuring is wasted if the answer is "buy it." If the answer is "we cannot buy right now," the room can focus on the lease pathway with full conviction.

Financial Capacity

NBIX has the resources — ~$478M net income in 2025, substantial cash position, strong balance sheet. Campus target: ~$600M (compressing with each strategic move). But NBIX just deployed $2.9B on Soleno (closing July 2026). Management wants to preserve dry powder for pipeline acquisitions tied to the 2027 drug readouts. The question is not whether NBIX can write the check — it is whether the board wants to write a $500M+ real estate check while digesting a $2.9B pharma acquisition and waiting on two drug readouts that could require additional capital.

Structural Viability

Six ownership structures have been modeled. Lead candidate: synthetic lease, variable rate, non-cash collateralized — gets the asset off balance sheet as an operating lease under ASC 842 while giving NBIX effective control. Gated on E&Y confirming operating lease treatment. If E&Y does not confirm, alternative structures all carry tradeoffs: cash collateralization ties up ~$604M as restricted cash, traditional purchase sits on the balance sheet, JV shares control.

Board Appetite

$15B+ market cap biopharma company whose board is focused on drug approvals, pipeline expansion, and Soleno integration. Asking a pharma board to approve a $500M+ real estate acquisition in the same year as a $2.9B pharma acquisition is a significant ask. The board's question: why now, and not after the 2027 readouts?

Buy Path

If NBIX can acquire now, the strategy simplifies:

  • Terminate B5 holdback (Decision 1 still applies)
  • Acquire campus through creditor-driven path or direct lender negotiation
  • Structure as synthetic lease (pending E&Y) or best available alternative
  • NBIX owns the asset, controls Lot 5, controls expansion, eliminates landlord risk permanently
  • No partner process, no ROFR choreography, no ghost buyer question
  • All downstream partner, ROFR, and lease-term questions become irrelevant

Lease Path

If NBIX cannot or will not buy now — the most likely near-term answer — the strategy shifts to the lease pathway. The governing question becomes: how much stability and control is NBIX willing to pay for today through extended lease term?

Not Yet, But Possibly Later

The most realistic answer. NBIX must structure the lease pathway to preserve the purchase option for the future. Partner selection becomes essential — because the right partner will agree to a purchase option upfront. The wrong partner, or an uncontrolled buyer, may never sell.

Why Terminate — The Strategic Logic▼

The landlord relationship is already adversarial. Any leverage NBIX holds should be exercised while it has value. The expansion option is leverage — termination converts it from a passive holding into an active strategic weapon.

  • Eliminates $68–73M NPV auto-extension liability — the single largest quantifiable risk on the campus

Any competent real estate advisor would reach this conclusion.

§10 Objection Analysis▼

§10.1 grants NBIX broad alteration rights. Landlord consent required only for Structural Alterations or changes affecting building systems. §10.1.2 requires Gemdale to identify "Specialty Alterations" within 10 business days — failure to respond is deemed approval. The densification submittal is designed to fall within NBIX's unilateral rights where possible, with any consent-required items structured to trigger the 10-business-day clock.

Gemdale's likely objections and responses have been mapped. JLL and Cooley are prepared to enforce §10 provisions if Gemdale withholds consent unreasonably.

Decision 3 — Ownership Pathway

How does the campus change hands?

Market intelligence indicates the voluntary sale pathway through Gemdale is effectively dead. Sophisticated buyers walk after counterparty diligence. Most probable: lender-driven resolution. However, both paths — and the scenario where Gemdale survives — must be mapped.

Key Definitions

Judicial Foreclosure: Lender files lawsuit → court judgment → public sale. 90–120 days. Allows deficiency judgment. Mechanism for lender to take title or designate buyer.

Short Sale: Owner sells below total debt, with lender consent. Liens released. IS a voluntary sale — ROFR triggers.

Reservation of Rights: Legal instrument: "we preserve enforcement rights at any time." Not a formal extension. No fixed end date.

Path A: Lender-Driven Resolution (Most Likely)

  1. Termination strips expansion narrative
  2. Gemdale position collapses — no refinancing story
  3. Lenders under reservation of rights, not formal extension. Enforcement at any time.
  4. Mezzanine lender has concentration risk and internal exit pressure. Motivated to resolve.
  5. Mezzanine initiates judicial foreclosure (90–120 days) or forces short sale
  6. Partner acquires from lender or through distressed process
  7. ROFR does NOT trigger — §40.4.1 carves out mortgagee sales
  8. Lease survives if SNDA in place
  9. Successor landlord not liable for prior acts (§16)

Path B: Gemdale Voluntary Sale (Low Probability)

ROFR triggers under §40.4.1. NBIX matches Material Terms. Risks:

  • Portfolio carve-out (Gate 6) — bundled sale kills ROFR
  • Upstream LLC transfer (Gate 7) — structural bypass
  • §40.6 extinguishment — 10-business-day window, miss it and rights die
Dominant Strategy — Correct in Every Scenario

Path C: Gemdale Survives — Why Termination Is Still the Right Move

The base case is Gemdale fails. But the strategy must be robust to survival. Critical finding: B5 termination is the right move regardless of which ownership outcome materializes. It is a dominant strategy.

Reason 1: Termination decreases the probability Gemdale survives▼

The expansion option is Gemdale's last credible marketing story. "Our anchor tenant is committed to expanding" is a powerful refinancing talking point. Termination removes it. The campus narrative collapses from growth campus to stabilized campus with a vacant lot. Makes refinancing harder, extension terms worse, survival narrower.

Reason 2: If Gemdale survives, termination gives NBIX a better negotiating position▼

The current option locks NBIX into 2022 pricing and the automatic 41–44 month auto-extension (§40.2.4) — the $68–73M liability. NBIX will never exercise under those terms. Once terminated, NBIX negotiates a new Lot 5 arrangement at current market rents, without the auto-extension, aligned with the 2027 readouts. If Gemdale survives and needs NBIX to expand to create value, NBIX negotiates from strength: Gemdale has a vacant lot with no other tenant prospects, a campus that only works as a single-tenant asset, and a tenant who is the only party that would ever build there.

Reason 3: Time is on NBIX's side — the note extension scenario▼

If Gemdale survives via a 24-month lender extension, three things happen:

  • Lease term burns down. Every month without extension, remaining duration shortens. Asset becomes less liquid, harder to finance. Campus value compresses further.
  • NBIX gets closer to readouts. 24-month extension from mid-2026 takes through mid-2028 — past the late 2027 readouts. By then, NBIX has pipeline clarity and can negotiate with conviction.
  • NBIX becomes more valuable to the owner. As remaining term shortens and value compresses, NBIX's willingness to extend becomes increasingly valuable. The 15-year commitment that is valuable today becomes even more valuable in 2028. NBIX leverage increases with every passing month.
Reason 4: Lot 5 remains NBIX's in every scenario▼

No third-party developer will spec-build 150,000 SF on a pad embedded in a single-tenant campus controlled by NBIX — especially in a soft market. ROFR (§40.4.1) and ROFO (§40.5) survive termination. The only party that will ever build on Lot 5 is NBIX.

Mezz Purchase — Evaluated Option (Back Pocket)

Under consideration for ~1 year. NBIX purchases mezzanine note (~$80M balance) + potential OZK paydown to ~65% LTV = ~$100–150M total. Gives NBIX control of foreclosure process. Deprioritized: requires NBIX to operate as real estate investor while pursuing drug approvals. Remains available if partner process fails or timeline compresses.

Risk of Not Controlling the Buyer: A new owner may not be a willing seller. A REIT or institutional fund acquiring as a core hold has no incentive to grant NBIX a purchase option. If NBIX wants the ability to own this campus in the future, that right is dramatically easier to negotiate upfront — as part of the acquisition — than after the fact with an owner who did not buy to sell.

The Governing Question

This strategy reduces to a single decision. Every path below flows from it.

How much stability and control is Neurocrine willing to pay for today — through extended lease term — versus waiting for pipeline clarity?

The "price" is not cash. NBIX is already paying $39.2M/year. A 15-year term does not change the annual number — it changes the duration. The "cost" is the optionality NBIX gives up: the ability to walk away in 2036 if the pipeline disappoints or strategy shifts.

Threshold question: Is Neurocrine in a position to buy the campus right now? If yes, the strategy simplifies dramatically. If not — the most likely near-term answer — the framework below applies. Either way, NBIX must continue to define the ultimate outcome in ownership/lease structure and flexibility 5–10+ years from now, and work backwards to execute the right plan.

Option A: Deploy Term Now

Commit the 15-year term as part of a partner acquisition. Secures stability, control, and favorable terms. Requires identifying and selecting the right partner — one willing to negotiate expansion options, purchase rights, and Lot 5 holdback upfront. Cost: locking in before 2027 readouts.

Risk: NBIX commits before knowing whether the pipeline supports growth at this scale.

Option B: Strategic Patience — Terminate and Wait

Terminate B5. Do nothing else. Let time and market forces compress campus value. Maintain all optionality. Strike a deal after 2027 readouts with maximum information and leverage.

No 15-year term. No partner process. No ownership discussions. No additional lease exposure. NBIX pays $39.2M/year under the existing lease through October 2036 and waits.

Why this works:

  • Campus value is a function of NBIX's term and willingness to expand. Both diminish over time. NBIX leverage increases.
  • Lot 5 is going nowhere. No independent market value. NBIX controls the timeline.
  • Gemdale situation resolves itself. Lease survives (if SNDA in place). Any new owner needs NBIX cooperation to create value.
  • Operational continuity: Under the NNN lease, day-to-day operations are unaffected by a landlord transition. Campus is 2–3 years old — structural risk near zero.

Risk: NBIX does not control the buyer. Purchase option may never materialize. See "Risk of Not Controlling the Buyer" in Decision 4.

Hybrid Option

Terminate B5. Run the partner identification process (screening, criteria, conversations). Pre-negotiate deal structure with preferred partner. But hold the 15-year term in reserve until after 2027 readouts. When readouts arrive, execute quickly because the deal is pre-structured — but term is the last card played, not the first.

Risk of Not Controlling the Buyer
If NBIX does not run the partner process, the eventual buyer could be a REIT or institutional fund that acquires as a core hold.
